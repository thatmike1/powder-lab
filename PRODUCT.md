# Product

## Register

product

## Users

People playing with a falling-sand sandbox in a browser. Tinkerers and the curious: fans of
cellular-automata toys, people who want to make something cool happen and watch it react. Primary
context is desktop with a mouse (touch supported). The session is short, idle, creative play:
"what happens if I drop lava on water," then showing a friend or grabbing a screenshot. They are
not at work, not completing a task; they are here to be delighted.

## Product Purpose

A falling-sand cellular-automaton sandbox. You paint materials (sand, water, lava, acid, fire,
plant, ice, and more) and watch them flow, burn, dissolve, freeze, and react through emergent
physics, with dynamic lighting and glow cast from fire and lava. It exists for delight and play,
not utility. Success looks like: someone loses a few minutes to it, goes "whoa," and wants to
share what they made.

## Brand Personality

Crafted, playful, tactile. The voice is a maker's toy built by someone who loves pixels: confident,
low-ego, no marketing fluff, no jargon. It should feel like a beloved little tool or handheld game,
something with care in every corner, not a product demo or a dashboard. Three words: crafted,
playful, tactile.

## Anti-references

- **Generic SaaS dashboard.** Cards everywhere, rounded-everything, slate-gray "dev tool" chrome,
  the safe corporate look. The simulation deserves better than a settings panel.
- **Childish / Comic-Sans toy.** Bubbly, over-rounded, primary-color "kids app" energy. This is
  playful, not juvenile.
- **Emoji as UI icons.** No glyph standing in for a control. Icons are hand-made.
- **"Obvious AI" reflexes for a pixel toy.** Gaussian-blur neon glow, smooth gradients,
  glassmorphism, tropey synthwave/CRT skins bolted onto the canvas. These read as machine-default
  and break the pixel medium.

## Design Principles

1. **The simulation is the star.** The chrome serves the canvas and recedes when in doubt. Every
   pixel of UI competes with the thing people actually came to watch.
2. **Pixel-native.** The interface is made of the same material as the canvas: crisp, grid-aligned,
   hand-crafted. Never a web-default frame wrapped around a pixel toy.
3. **Coherence over decoration.** The UI reflects the sim. The accent color follows the material
   you have selected; light is dithered the way the pixels are, not blurred. The chrome borrows the
   canvas's own language.
4. **Craft over convention.** Reject the first obvious answer (the SaaS card, the neon bloom) in
   favor of the considered, made-by-hand one. If it looks like it could have been generated by
   default, it has failed.
5. **Playful, not juvenile.** It is a toy, and it should feel like one, but a toy with taste.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

- Materials are never identified by color alone: every material carries a name and a keyboard key,
  so the palette is usable without relying on hue (matters for color-vision differences, since the
  swatches are intentionally close in places, e.g. the grays).
- Maintain legible contrast for text and icons on the warm-dark chrome.
- Honor `prefers-reduced-motion` for decorative chrome motion (animated selection marquee, toggle
  transitions). The simulation itself is the core content and is the user's to start and stop.
- Keep interactive targets comfortable for touch.
- Not chasing a formal WCAG grade as a release gate, but the interface should stay readable and
  operable for a casual, mixed audience.
